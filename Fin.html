  • Financial Services — banking, factoring, leasing, merchant banking

3.1 Factoring
HIGH ★★★ 2 + 10 marks
"Factoring is a financial arrangement where a business sells its accounts receivable (invoices) to a third party called a Factor at a discount to get immediate cash."
3 Parties in Factoring
  • Client (Seller): Business that sells its invoices
  • Factor: Financial institution that buys the invoices
  • Debtor (Customer): Who owes payment to the client
7 Types of Factoring (Professor tested all in MCQs)
  • 1. Recourse Factoring: Client bears risk of non-payment. If debtor defaults, client repays factor.
  • 2. Non-Recourse Factoring: Factor bears risk. Client has no liability if debtor defaults.
  • 3. Domestic Factoring: All parties (client, factor, debtor) in same country.
  • 4. Export/International Factoring: Cross-border; exporter gets immediate payment for overseas invoices.
  • 5. Advance Factoring: Factor pays 70–90% of invoice upfront; balance after collection minus fee.
  • 6. Maturity Factoring: Factor pays on maturity date regardless of whether debtor has paid.
  • 7. Full-Service Factoring: Includes financing + credit assessment + receivables management.

3.2 Leasing
HIGH 2 + 10 marks
"Leasing is a contractual arrangement where the owner (Lessor) allows another party (Lessee) to use an asset for a specified period in exchange for regular payments, without transferring ownership."
Types of Leasing
  • Operating Lease: Short-term. Lessor retains ownership + risks/rewards. Lessee treats payments as expense. Example: Leasing printers for 3 years.
  • Finance Lease (Capital Lease): Long-term. Lessee assumes most risks/rewards. Recorded as asset + liability on lessee's balance sheet. Example: Leasing machinery for 10 years.
  • Sale and Leaseback: Owner sells asset to buyer, then leases it back. Gets immediate cash but retains use. Example: Company sells office building, leases it back for 15 years.
  • Direct Lease: Lessor directly provides asset to lessee
  • Cross-Border Lease: Parties in different countries

4.3 Non-Performing Assets (NPA)
HIGH ★★★ 2 + 10 marks
"A Non-Performing Asset (NPA) is a loan or advance where the borrower has not paid interest or principal for more than 90 days."
Classification of NPAs
  • Sub-Standard Asset: NPA for less than 12 months
  • Doubtful Asset: NPA for more than 12 months
  • Loss Asset: Identified as loss by bank/auditor; uncollectible
Causes of NPA
  • Weak credit appraisal by banks
  • Economic slowdown reducing borrower income
  • Wilful default — borrower can pay but refuses to
  • Diversion of loan funds to other purposes
  • Political interference in lending decisions
  • Loan evergreening — hiding bad loans with new loans
Impact of NPAs
  • Reduces bank profitability
  • Weakens capital adequacy ratio
  • Reduces credit availability in economy
  • Erodes public confidence in banks
NPA threshold = 90 days. This number WILL appear in a 2-mark question. Never forget: Sub-Standard → Doubtful → Loss (SDS).
4.4 SARFAESI Act 2002
HIGH ★★★ 2 + 10 marks
"SARFAESI = Securitisation and Reconstruction of Financial Assets and Enforcement of Security Interest Act, 2002. Allows banks to recover NPAs WITHOUT going to court."
Prior to SARFAESI
  • Banks had to file cases in civil courts — very slow process
  • RDBI Act 1993 created Debt Recovery Tribunals (DRTs) — still slow
  • NPAs kept increasing — SARFAESI was the solution
Objectives of SARFAESI
  • Recover NPAs without court intervention
  • Facilitate securitisation of financial assets
  • Empower Asset Reconstruction Companies (ARCs)
  • Faster resolution of stressed assets
SARFAESI Process — Step by Step
  1. Loan classified as NPA
  2. Bank issues 60-day notice under Section 13(2)
  3. Borrower can raise objection in writing
  4. Bank must reply to objection within 1 week (Section 13(3A))
  5. If still unpaid → bank takes physical possession of secured asset
  6. Bank can sell, lease, or auction the asset
  7. Proceeds: First → costs, Second → bank dues, Third → surplus to borrower
Important Sections
  • Section 13(2): 60-day notice to borrower
  • Section 13(3A): Bank's reply to borrower's objection within 1 week
  • Section 6(1): Acquisition of asset by securitisation company

4.5 Securitisation
HIGH ★★★ 2 + 10 marks
"Securitisation is the process of converting illiquid long-term assets (loans/receivables) into tradeable marketable securities issued against them."
6 Parties in Securitisation
  • Originator: Bank/FI holding the loans
  • SPV (Special Purpose Vehicle): Trust that buys and holds the assets
  • Investment Banker: Structures the entire deal
  • Credit Rating Agency: Rates the securities issued
  • RPA (Receiving & Paying Agent): Servicing agent
  • Investors: Buy the securities
5 Stages of Securitisation
  1. Identification: Pool of eligible assets identified by originator
  2. Transfer: Assets transferred to SPV
  3. Issue: SPV issues securities to investors
  4. Redemption: Securities repaid from asset cash flows
  5. Credit Rating: Rating of issued securities
Advantages of Securitisation
  • Additional source of funds
  • Removes NPAs from bank's balance sheet
  • Improves Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR)
  • Spreading credit risk among investors
  • Lower cost of funding
  • Higher rate of return for investors
Securitisation in India
  • First attempt: Citibank securitised ICICI receivables, February 1991 — ₹15 crore raised
  • Not popular due to: heavy stamp duty, cumbersome transfer procedures, absence of standardised documentation
  • Future bright due to liberalisation and capital market growth

4.9 Banking Scams in India
HIGH 10 marks
"A banking scam is a deliberate financial fraud involving manipulation of banking systems or regulatory loopholes, resulting in unauthorised financial gain and systemic loss."
Types of Banking Scams
  • Loan/Credit Frauds: Biggest by value. Fake documents, inflated financials, diversion of funds. (₹1,000–₹10,000 crore per case)
  • Cyber/Digital Frauds: Fastest growing. OTP theft, phishing, fake apps, UPI scams.
  • KYC/Identity Frauds: Fake Aadhaar/PAN, duplicate accounts, mule accounts.
  • Corporate/Guarantee Frauds: Fake Letters of Undertaking (LoUs), SWIFT misuse.
  • Loan Evergreening: New loan to hide old bad loan. Creates illusion of healthy books.
PNB/Nirav Modi Scam — Case Study (Professor gave full PPT)
  • Scale: ₹11,000+ crore
  • Method: Fake Letters of Undertaking (LoUs) issued to overseas banks
  • SWIFT system not integrated with core banking system — exploited this gap
  • Loan rollover (evergreening) used to hide fraud for years
  • Impact: Led to SWIFT-CBS integration reform in Indian banks
Systemic Causes
  • Weak internal controls and poor auditing
  • Political interference in loan approvals
  • Collusion between bank officials and fraudsters
  • Manual approval loopholes

5.1 Foreign Exchange Market
MEDIUM 2 + 10 marks
"Foreign exchange (forex) refers to the global marketplace for buying and selling national currencies to facilitate international trade, investment, and financial transactions."
Types of Forex Markets
  • Spot Market: Immediate exchange of currencies at current rate
  • Forward Market: Agreement to buy/sell at a future date at pre-agreed price
  • Futures Market: Standardised contracts traded on exchanges
  • Options Market: Right (not obligation) to buy/sell at specific price
Market Participants
  • Central Banks: Control monetary policy and intervene to stabilise currency
  • Commercial Banks: Handle large volumes of currency trading
  • Investment Managers: Speculative trading
  • Retail Traders: Individuals speculating on currency movements
India's Exchange Rate System
  • India uses a Managed Floating Exchange Rate system
  • Rupee value primarily determined by market forces (demand & supply)
  • RBI intervenes to prevent excessive volatility
  • Historical: INR 4.76/USD (1947) → ~₹84/USD (2024)
  • India's Forex Reserves: ~$693 billion (among world's largest)
Balance of Payments (BOP)
  • Surplus BOP = Foreign currency inflow > outflow → strong domestic currency
  • Deficit BOP = Outflow > inflow → weak domestic currency

6.1 Prevention of Money Laundering Act (PMLA) 2002
HIGH 2 + 10 marks
"Money laundering is the process of making illegally obtained money appear legitimate by passing it through a series of transactions."
3 Stages of Money Laundering
  1. Placement: Placing illegal funds into the financial system (cash deposits, cash purchases)
  2. Layering: Disguising the trail through complex transactions (wire transfers, shell companies)
  3. Integration: Reintroducing laundered money into the legitimate economy (buying property, businesses)
PMLA 2002 — Key Provisions
  • Enacted to prevent and control money laundering
  • Enforcement Directorate (ED) is the primary investigating agency
  • Banks and FIs must maintain records and report suspicious transactions
  • Attachment and confiscation of proceeds of crime
  • India reported: 5,200+ money laundering cases by ED since 2014
  • 6,300+ total PMLA cases investigated till 2025
Methods Used in Money Laundering
  • Shell companies: Fake companies to rotate and hide money
  • Loan Evergreening: Using new loans to hide bad debt
  • Trade-Based Money Laundering (TBML): Over/under-invoicing imports and exports
  • Hawala: Informal value transfer system outside banking
